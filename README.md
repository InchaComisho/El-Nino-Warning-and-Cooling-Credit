# El Niño in the Age of Global Warming Is a Warning from the Earth — How Do We Cool the Heat Stored in the Ocean?

> El Niño is a natural phenomenon.
> But El Niño in the age of global warming is not the same as past El Niño events.
> It occurs on top of an ocean and atmosphere that are already warmer.

---

## Conclusion: The Next El Niño Is Not the Same as Past El Niño Events

El Niño is part of the natural variability of the Earth system.

However, El Niño in the age of global warming should be understood from a different baseline. The ocean and atmosphere are already warmer than they were during many past events, and more than 90% of the excess heat accumulated in the climate system has been absorbed by the ocean.

When an El Niño event is added on top of an already heat-loaded ocean, the result may be more than a temporary warm year. It can amplify risks across heat waves, marine heat waves, droughts, heavy rainfall, wildfire, coral bleaching, ecosystem disruption, food stress, and water stress.

This document treats El Niño not as a single disaster prediction, but as a warning signal from a warming ocean and a weakening planetary cooling system.

---

## What Is El Niño?

El Niño is a natural climate phenomenon in which sea-surface temperatures in the central and eastern equatorial Pacific become warmer than normal.

When El Niño develops, atmospheric circulation changes and weather patterns can shift across many regions. Depending on location, this can influence heat, drought, heavy rain, floods, tropical cyclone behavior, agriculture, marine ecosystems, fisheries, and wildfire risk.

The problem is not El Niño itself. The problem is that today’s El Niño events occur on top of a planet whose baseline temperature, ocean heat content, and ecological stress are already higher.

---

## Why El Niño in the Age of Global Warming Is More Dangerous

In the past, strong El Niño events occurred on a cooler planetary baseline.

Today, the atmosphere, land, and ocean have already warmed. The ocean is especially important because it has absorbed most of the excess heat in the climate system. That means the ocean is not merely a surface temperature indicator; it is a vast heat reservoir.

When El Niño raises sea-surface temperatures on top of that stored heat, the risk is not only a statistical temperature anomaly. It is a physical stacking of heat on an already stressed Earth system.

From a pre-verification perspective, this is a reason to treat the next strong El Niño not only as a meteorological event, but as a climate-risk signal.

---

## Why It Is Different from El Niño Events More Than a Decade Ago

Past strong El Niño events already caused major impacts: heat waves, droughts, heavy rainfall, wildfire, agricultural losses, fishery disruption, coral bleaching, food-price pressure, water stress, and ecosystem damage.

Since then, the baseline has continued to shift. Sea-surface temperatures have risen, land temperatures have risen, urban waste heat has increased, forest and soil water-retention capacity has weakened in many places, and ocean circulation and ecosystems face growing stress.

Therefore, even if the El Niño mechanism is natural, the conditions into which it arrives are not the same. A similar event can produce larger impacts when the surrounding system is already heat-loaded and less able to cool itself.

---

## La Niña May No Longer Cool Enough

La Niña often follows or alternates with El Niño. It is associated with cooler-than-normal sea-surface temperatures in the equatorial Pacific.

In past periods, La Niña sometimes appeared to moderate the heat raised by El Niño. But in a warmer climate, La Niña may no longer cool enough to reset the system in the same way.

Even when surface air temperatures temporarily ease, heat can remain stored in the ocean. If another El Niño then develops, the next heat pulse may start from a higher baseline.

This is why the issue is not a single abnormal year. The deeper concern is the accumulation of heat and the weakening of natural cooling functions.

---

## What We Should Watch Is Not Only Average Temperature

Average global temperature matters, but it is not enough to describe the state of the Earth system.

We should also watch whether the planet’s cooling functions are still working:

- Can the ocean circulate nutrients and heat?
- Is the upper ocean storing too much heat?
- Can plankton, coral, and fisheries remain within viable temperature ranges?
- Can forests hold water and cool the air through evapotranspiration?
- Can soils receive rain, retain moisture, and support microbes and insects?
- Are cities adding too much waste heat?
- Is WBGT moving beyond safe human activity limits?

The Earth is not a machine controlled by CO2 concentration alone. It is a circulation system of heat, water, soil, forests, oceans, and life.

---

## Preventing a Super El Niño Requires Reducing Heat

Humans cannot fully control whether a strong El Niño occurs.

But we can reduce some of the conditions that make its impacts worse. The core task is to reduce heat load and restore cooling functions.

Emissions reduction remains necessary. But emissions reduction alone does not quickly remove heat already stored in the ocean, land, and built environment.

Cooling contribution can include:

- supporting ocean circulation and marine ecosystems
- protecting plankton foundations
- restoring forest evapotranspiration
- returning organic matter to soils
- increasing humus and soil moisture
- storing rainwater and restoring watershed function
- reducing urban heat and waste heat
- lowering WBGT
- using mist and evaporative cooling where dry-hot conditions make it appropriate
- combining rainwater, soil, dehumidification, and airflow strategies in humid regions

These are not merely environmental gestures. They are potential cooling contributions to the planetary heat balance.

---

## What We Need Is Thermal Accounting, Not Only Carbon Accounting

Current climate policy is primarily designed around CO2 emissions.

Carbon accounting and carbon credits usually evaluate emissions reduction, removal, or offsetting. These are important, but they do not directly answer the next question: how much heat has actually been reduced, avoided, or dissipated?

Thermal accounting asks:

- How much urban heat was reduced?
- How much did WBGT improve?
- How much soil moisture was restored?
- How much rainwater was retained?
- How much evapotranspiration was recovered in forests or green areas?
- How much ocean-circulation or ecosystem cooling function was supported?
- How much regional cooling capacity was restored?

This is the conceptual space in which Cooling Credits become relevant.

---

## Carbon Credit to Cooling Credit Transition

Carbon credits are designed mainly around emissions reduction, removal, or offset accounting. These remain important, but they do not directly measure whether ocean heat, land heat, WBGT, surface temperature, soil moisture, evapotranspiration, or natural cooling functions have improved.

In the age of global warming, El Niño should be understood as a heat-stacking warning. The policy question is no longer only "How much CO2 was offset?" It is also "How much heat load was physically reduced or buffered?"

Cooling Credits are proposed as a transition from carbon accounting alone toward thermal accounting and measurable cooling contribution.

The heat-inertia problem is central. Emissions reduction is necessary, but it mainly works as a brake on additional heating. It does not immediately remove heat already stored in the ocean, land, built environment, soil, and water-vapor system.

The Earth does not cool because emissions are offset on paper. It cools only when heat input is reduced, existing heat load is physically lowered or buffered, and natural cooling functions are restored.

This is also a cost-effectiveness problem. Carbon credits can support accounting, compliance, and some mitigation finance, but carbon-credit-centered policy has not demonstrated sufficient cost-effectiveness as the primary climate-finance mechanism if it improves accounting scores while physical heat-load indicators remain high.

## What Is Cooling Credit?

Cooling Credit is a proposed framework that assigns value to measurable cooling contribution.

Where carbon credits mainly evaluate emissions reductions or offsets, Cooling Credits focus on whether an action measurably contributes to cooling or to the recovery of cooling functions.

Possible evaluation targets include:

- air-temperature reduction
- surface-temperature reduction
- WBGT reduction
- waste-heat reduction
- water-cycle recovery
- rainwater retention
- soil-moisture recovery
- humus production
- forest and green-space evapotranspiration recovery
- ocean-circulation support
- marine ecosystem protection
- regional cooling potential

Cooling Credit is not a license to emit. It is proof of measurable cooling contribution.

---

## The Best Cooling Model Differs by Climate Zone

Cooling Credit matters because the best cooling action differs by region.

Mist cooling may be highly effective in dry-hot regions, while its effect can weaken in very humid areas. In humid and rainy regions, rainwater retention, humus, soil moisture, ventilation, dehumidification, and water-cycle recovery may be more important.

From a conceptual framework perspective:

- humid and rainy regions may benefit from rainwater, humus, and soil-water retention
- hot and humid cities may need dehumidification, airflow, shade, and WBGT reduction
- dry-hot regions may benefit from evaporative mist cooling
- tropical cities may require soil-water and hydrological cooling models

Cooling Credit should therefore not evaluate only one cooling technology. It should evaluate locally appropriate cooling actions based on humidity, rainfall, soil moisture, solar radiation, seasonal conditions, and risk.

---

## Society Will Not Move Unless Cooling Action Has Value

Many people experience climate action as cost, restraint, or burden.

That framing is not enough to move society at scale. What is needed is an institutional design in which cooling the Earth has value.

Such a system can reward:

- companies that reduce urban heat
- municipalities that store and circulate rainwater
- agriculture that restores soil moisture
- forests treated as water-retention and evapotranspiration infrastructure
- ocean circulation and ecosystem recovery as investment targets
- buildings and urban design that reduce WBGT

If cooling action has value, climate response can become a market for regeneration rather than only a burden.

---

## Turning the El Niño Warning into Institutional Design

El Niño is a warning signal from the Earth system.

In the age of global warming, a strong El Niño can reveal how far civilization has moved away from natural circulation.

The warning should not end as a weather record. It should become institutional design:

```text
from heat-emitting civilization
to heat-reducing civilization

from economies that break circulation
to economies that restore circulation

from carbon accounting alone
to thermal accounting
```

Cooling Credits are one candidate institution for that transition.

---

## The Future Is Not Yet Fixed

El Niño in the age of global warming asks a practical question:

Can civilization still move toward restoring natural cooling functions, or will it keep treating warning signals as temporary weather anomalies?

The answer is not fixed.

The Earth is not a machine. It is a living circulation system. Restoring that circulation is one of the central climate tasks of this century.

Cooling Credits are proposed as one way to evaluate and reward that restoration.
