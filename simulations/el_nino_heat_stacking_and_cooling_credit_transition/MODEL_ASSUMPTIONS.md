# Model Assumptions

This simulation is intentionally simple. It is designed to clarify causal logic, not to forecast climate outcomes.

## Scope

- Period: 2015-2035
- Unit: normalized index from 0 to 100
- Method: deterministic counterfactual scenario model
- Purpose: compare carbon-accounting-centered and thermal-accounting-centered policy pathways

## What the Model Assumes

1. El Nino pulses add temporary heat stress on top of background ocean and land heat loads.
2. Global warming raises the background heat-load baseline over time.
3. Carbon-credit policy improves emissions accounting and mitigation incentives, but it has only an indirect and delayed effect on already accumulated heat.
4. Cooling-credit policy directs finance toward physical cooling functions such as urban heat reduction, WBGT reduction, soil moisture recovery, water-cycle recovery, forest evapotranspiration, ocean-circulation support, and marine ecosystem protection.
5. Natural cooling functions reduce heat-stacking risk when they recover together rather than separately.
6. Cooling Credit pathways reduce heat amplification risk; they do not stop El Nino.
7. Heat inertia and climate time lag can keep physical heat-load indexes high even while emissions-accounting indexes improve.

## What the Model Does Not Claim

- It does not predict ENSO timing or intensity.
- It does not estimate real ocean heat content in joules.
- It does not replace coupled climate models.
- It does not prove that any specific intervention is universally effective.
- It does not say carbon credits are useless.

## Policy Interpretation

Carbon credits can support emissions reduction and accounting. However, carbon accounting alone does not directly answer whether ocean heat, land heat, WBGT, surface temperature, soil moisture, evapotranspiration, water-cycle recovery, or marine cooling functions have improved.

Cooling Credits are proposed to add the missing measurement layer: thermal accounting for real cooling contribution.

The Earth does not cool because emissions are offset on paper. It cools only when heat input is reduced, existing heat load is physically lowered or buffered, and natural cooling functions are restored.
